What do You Want to Make Real?

Monday Morning Homepage is an ongoing prototype exploring imaginative frameworks surrounding climate change. When the facts of reality paint a picture of severe global collapse, we need to find a way to cultivate hope for the future, for our species, and for our planet.


This website is a digital artifact from a better, but possible future. The same way a newspaper from the past gives us a glimpse into the goings-on and lives of its moment, Monday Morning Homepage is a window into the lives of a world where we adapted to and overcame the climate crisis.


Much like change in the real world, this website centers the local actions and happenings within a community. How can we take some of the concepts we see here and apply them to our own worlds?


If you are interested in contributing content, writing, or ideas to Monday Morning Homepage, please feel free to submit an issue on our GitHub, or email gtenino@ucsd.edu .

Opinion: Failure of Las Amigas Jardineras Housing Projects; The Pitfalls of Lowest-Bidder Carbon Policy and Ethical Neo-Globalism.

August 16, 2085 – A pressing buzzing fills the air in the brand new Las Amigas Jardineras neighborhood designed by the rockstar city planning firm, Ayacucho STEPS. This neighborhood represents the bleeding edge of global urban design. Wind-catchers fan out into the sky, following complex, computer-generated curves. Citrus and olive trees are pain-stakingly coaxed along narrow, walkable, city streets. The ground is slightly spongy underfoot, a mix of ground-up automobile tires and genetically engineered mycelium designed to self-heal by filtering microplastics out of runoff. On a sweltering 110 degree day, the temperature on the shaded streets of Las Amigas is 78 degrees with absolutely zero electricity use. Yet, despite the world-class design and over 2000 residents, we saw almost nobody on the street. Las Amigas Jardineras has a huge problem: mosquitos.

Tucson municipal planning directive 7 outlines the guidelines for selecting a community development proposal during the procurement process. Sub-section 7.1.1 states the following:

  1. Limiting lifetime greenhouse gas emissions per housed person must be a priority during the selection process of the assigned planning committee,

  2. and any positive lifetime greenhouse gas emissions must directly contribute to societal well-being and demonstrate value in significant excess to the great, societal cost of those emissions.

  3. Greenhouse gas emissions and analysis of societal value must conform to proof-standards outlined in section 19.1.


In practice, proving “value in significant excess” is very difficult outside the medical and military fields. This public directive forces a type of lowest–bidder process regarding carbon, in the planning committees. Over the past 48 years since municipal secession, nine different public proposals have unsuccessfully attempted to amend this directive to allow greater flexibility for public comfort. Yet, our society still seems unready to make such a change. The societal trauma of our grandparent’s climate crisis runs very, very deep.

In the final selection process for the development, two main design firms presented competing proposals. The Pascua Yaqui tribal corporation, Planificadores de un Futuro Mejor en el nombre de nuestro Señor de Los Milagros (PFM), and the world-renowned Medellín-based design union Ayacucho STEPS. PFM’s proposal involved neo-native pueblo-type designs, along with several open-source Iranian windcatcher designs specifically designed with the desert in mind. PFM representatives also correctly predicted the potential issues with mosquitos that Ayacucho would face. According to the planning committee’s final report, PFM’s proposal represented a “more realistic, sustainable, and diverse quality of life” than the Columbian designer’s union could ever offer.


Yet, Ayacucho STEPS still won the proposal on the grounds that it had 47% lower greenhouse emissions than that of PFM. Unfortunately, PFM’s methane-powered rammed-earth construction equipment needed to be shipped by road from Santa-Fe. PFM needed most of their production to be performed locally, without the efficiency of economies of scale. Ten years ago, their proposal would have been very carbon-competitive, but the world has changed with the introduction of global rock-star design firms.


Ayacucho prefabricates the buildings in Tegucigalpa, cultivates the GMO mycelium in Shenzhen, force-grows the olive trees in Brasília, and 3d-prints the wind catchers in Düsseldorf. Everything ships in on electric trains and wingsail cargo ships. Modern global supply lines like this eerily echo the days of wanton consumption and unfettered capitalism; plastic toys and gadgets shipped across the world in giant diesel-powered ships and fleets of dirty trucks, only to be briefly used and shipped back as trash to the neo-colonized nation states.


Yet despite how it feels emotionally, holistic economic science shows us that the vast global networks of wingsail cargo ships, solar trains, and packet-blimps *can* have significantly lower emissions and resource use than doing things locally. But this isn’t the whole story, we have to ask what it means for new-society when our social policies increasingly encourage centralized thinking. Local sourcing brings resilience and adaptation to the unique realities of our ecosystems and environments. Ayacucho STEPS is working on 8 different design projects outside of their biome. Their artistry, efficiency, and expertise is world class. But we can’t expect designers in Medellín to understand the unique ways that our desert would interact with their work better than a design firm staffed predominantly native peoples who have lived on this land since ancient times. What we gain in carbon-efficiency by outsourcing, we lose in local wisdom.


It is well understood that public policy must be made with care for the complexities of the human ecosystem. However, carbon regulations are often exceptions to these guidelines. The rigidity and asceticism with which we’ve driven emissions down has brought us to a safer place on this Earth. But strict, straightforward rules often bring us towards patterns that we neither want nor expect. Patterns like neo-globalization. We ought to bring holistic ecological considerations to our carbon-policy and try to consider the myriad effects of a single action in a complex world. We ought to consider the mosquitos.
